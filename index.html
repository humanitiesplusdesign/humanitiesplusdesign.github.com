Ink

Ink builds on previous work with Corrispondenza, Inquiry, and Fineo, combining aspects of all of them into one tool to explore a single correspondence collection.

Corrispondenza

Corrispondenza was our first tool to expand the usefulness of mapping correspondence by showing all of the letters (by year) which could not be plotted on the map. For letters where we are missing source or destination coordinates, a count appears in gray on the timeline. For letters that are missing dates, the link between the cities is plotted, but the letter is not counted in the timeline.

Inquiry

Inquiry was developed as a starting point for visual browsing of a very large data set. The idea is to combine in one interface multiple points of entry. Again, the data is taken from 16th & 17th c correspondence. It allows scholars to navigate the data set by archive source, by place, by time, by person. For each category, Inquiry tracks missing or uncertain data that cannot be plotted.

Fineo

Fineo is the name given by DensityDesign, but Mapping the Republic of Letters researchers affectionately refer to the tool as the multi-dimensional relation viewer. Based on the Sankey flow diagram developed by Matthew Henry Phineas Riall Sankey, Fineo shows relationships between multiple dimensions of data. Mapping the Republic of Letters first used Fineo to explore the intellectual sub-communities among Athanasius Kircher's correspondents.
